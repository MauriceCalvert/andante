# Baroque Fugue Subject Archetypes

Catalogue of subject types distilled from WTC I & II (Bach), the organ
fugues (Bach), HWV 605–610 and Op. 6 concerti grossi (Handel), Corelli
Op. 5 & 6 sonata da chiesa fugal movements, Vivaldi concerto fugues
(L'estro armonico and later), Telemann Fugues légères and solo violin
fantasias, and D. Scarlatti fugal sonatas.

Each archetype defines a **contour template**, a **rhythmic profile**,
a **harmonic implication**, and an **affect affinity**. The generator
instantiates an archetype by choosing specific pitches within the
contour constraints, binding rhythm to contour, and checking answer
and fragmentation viability.

The three-part subject structure (from Open Music Theory) governs all
archetypes:

```
Kopfmotiv (head) → Fortspinnung (continuation) → Kadenz (cadential close)
```

The head is the memorable gesture. The continuation spins out the
energy. The close returns to stability. Every archetype specifies all
three parts.

## 1. SCALAR — Stepwise running subject

**Source examples:** BWV 846 (C major, WTC I), BWV 847 (C minor, WTC I),
BWV 952, Corelli Op. 5 No. 1 Vivace, Vivaldi RV 565 (L'estro armonico
No. 11), Telemann Fantasia No. 2 fugue.

**Contour:** Predominantly stepwise motion, ascending or descending,
covering a 5th to octave. May begin with a single held note or repeated
note before the run begins (BWV 846 pattern). Direction is typically
unidirectional in the head, with possible reversal in the Fortspinnung.

**Rhythmic profile:** Even note values dominating — crotchets (BWV 846,
grave style) or quavers/semiquavers (Vivaldi, energetic style). The
evenness *is* the character. Rhythmic variety comes from the tail, not
the run. The Kadenz may slow (augment the final two notes) or accelerate
into the next entry.

**Harmonic implication:** Clear tonic–dominant polarity. Ascending from
1 to 5 (or descending from 5 to 1) gives a natural tonal answer point.
Each step implies a passing chord.

**Affect affinity:** Majestaet (slow scalar = solemn weight),
Entschlossenheit (fast scalar = driving momentum), Sehnsucht
(minor scalar = brooding intensity).

**Fragmentation:** Scalar subjects fragment beautifully — any contiguous
segment is a usable episode cell. The head (first 3–4 notes) makes a
strong sequential unit.

**Answer type:** Typically tonal — the 1→5 or 5→1 boundary is explicit
in a scalar subject.

## 2. TRIADIC — Arpeggiated / chord-outline subject

**Source examples:** BWV 848 (C# major, WTC I — 6ths), BWV 862
(Ab major, WTC I), Handel Op. 6 No. 1 Allegro, Handel HWV 605
(G minor fugue), Corelli Op. 6 concerti grossi fugal movements.

**Contour:** Opens with broken-chord motion — ascending or descending
triad, sometimes extended to a 7th. The Fortspinnung typically fills
the gap with stepwise contrary motion (gap-fill principle). Range is
wider than scalar: octave to 10th.

**Rhythmic profile:** The arpeggio notes are typically longer (crotchets
or dotted crotchets) establishing the harmonic pillars; the gap-fill is
shorter values (quavers or semiquavers). This long-short contrast is the
rhythmic signature.

**Harmonic implication:** Each arpeggio note *is* a chord tone — the
harmony is explicit, not implied. The subject outlines I then pivots
toward V. Handel's subjects are particularly triadic and harmonically
direct.

**Affect affinity:** Zaertlichkeit/Zierlich (gentle broken chord),
Majestaet (wide arpeggio = grandeur), Verwunderung (fast arpeggio
= virtuosic display).

**Fragmentation:** The arpeggio head is a strong episode cell. The
gap-fill tail is a second independent cell. Two-cell episodes are
natural.

**Answer type:** Depends on whether the arpeggio crosses the 1–5
boundary. An ascending 1-3-5 head requires tonal mutation (5 becomes 1
in the answer). A descending 5-3-1 head may use a real answer.

## 3. CHROMATIC — Semitone-inflected subject

**Source examples:** BWV 849 (C# minor, WTC I — chromatic descent),
BWV 853 (Eb minor/D# minor, WTC I — minor 9th leap + chromatic),
BWV 869 (B minor, WTC I — all 12 pitch classes), Scarlatti K.30
("Cat's Fugue"), Handel Op. 6 No. 12 Andante (inverted Scarlatti).

**Contour:** Contains at least one chromatic inflection — a raised or
lowered degree that doesn't belong to the diatonic scale. Common
patterns: chromatic descent (lamento bass figure), chromatic neighbour
tone, or chromatic approach to a structural tone. BWV 869 is the extreme
case (all 12 pitches). The typical chromatic subject has 2–3 chromatic
notes in an otherwise diatonic line.

**Rhythmic profile:** Tends toward longer note values (minims, crotchets)
to give the chromatic inflections weight and audibility. Fast chromatic
runs exist (toccata style) but are less common in fugue subjects.
The slow chromatic descent is the signature rhythm: equal values,
grave tempo.

**Harmonic implication:** Chromatic subjects imply richer harmony —
secondary dominants, diminished chords, Neapolitan approaches. The
cross-relation risk is highest here. The answer must handle the
chromatic alterations carefully.

**Affect affinity:** Klage/Dolore (descending chromatic = grief),
Sehnsucht (chromatic tension = gravity), Zorn (expressive
chromaticism = anguish). This is the affect-richest archetype.

**Fragmentation:** Chromatic cells are powerful episode material but
dangerous — repeated chromatic sequences can lose the key. Best used
sparingly.

**Answer type:** Often tonal, with mutations at chromatic boundary
crossings. Real answers can produce ugly cross-relations.

## 4. RHYTHMIC — Defined by its rhythm more than its pitches

**Source examples:** BWV 850 (D major, WTC I — dotted rhythm),
BWV 856 (F major, WTC I), Handel Op. 6 No. 7 Allegro (repeated-note
accelerando: 14 repetitions of one pitch in accelerating values),
Handel Op. 6 No. 5 fugue, Telemann Fugues légères,
Vivaldi RV 120 finale.

**Contour:** Narrow pitch range (4th to 6th). The melodic content is
secondary to the rhythmic figure. Common: repeated notes (Handel No. 7
is the extreme — same pitch repeated in progressively shorter values),
dotted rhythms (French overture style), syncopated entries, or
"hammer-blow" motifs (Telemann/Handel borrowing).

**Rhythmic profile:** The rhythm IS the subject. Distinctive patterns:
dotted quaver–semiquaver (French), short-short-long (lombardic),
repeated-note accelerando (Handel), hemiola figures (3 against 2).
The Fortspinnung maintains the rhythmic figure while the pitch moves.

**Harmonic implication:** Because the pitch content is narrow, the
harmonic implication is ambiguous — the subject sits on one harmony
for several beats before moving. This makes the subject harmonically
flexible (can fit over different bass harmonies in different entries).

**Affect affinity:** Entschlossenheit (driving repeated notes),
Freudigkeit (playful dotted rhythms), Majestaet (French overture
dots = ceremonial), Zorn (martial repeated notes).

**Fragmentation:** The rhythmic cell is the episode unit, not the pitch
content. Episodes transpose the rhythm over different scale degrees.

**Answer type:** Usually real — narrow-range subjects rarely cross the
tonic–dominant boundary.

---

## 5. COMPOUND — Two contrasting gestures in one subject

**Source examples:** BWV 578 (G minor, "Little Fugue" — held note +
scale run), BWV 861 (G minor, WTC I), BWV 854 (E major, WTC I —
leap + trill + scale), Vivaldi RV 210 finale ("jumpy and jagged"),
Corelli Op. 5 No. 3 Allegro.

**Contour:** Two distinct sections: a *static* opening (held note,
repeated note, or slow arpeggio) followed by a *kinetic* continuation
(scale run, sequential figuration, or virtuosic passage). The contrast
between the two halves is the subject's identity. BWV 578: held D then
descending scale. BWV 854: upward leap then ornamental descent.

**Rhythmic profile:** Bipartite: long values (minim, dotted crotchet)
in the head, short values (quavers, semiquavers) in the tail. The
rhythmic acceleration from head to tail creates forward momentum.
The Kadenz either continues the kinetic energy or abruptly stops
(half-note arrival).

**Harmonic implication:** The held/repeated head establishes the tonic
firmly. The kinetic tail traverses harmonic territory — typically
moving from I toward V. The tonic–dominant journey is stretched across
the subject's two halves.

**Affect affinity:** Majestaet (solemn held note → dramatic run),
Dolore (sustained tension → release), Verwunderung (static →
virtuosic).

**Fragmentation:** The head and tail are natural independent episode
cells. The head can be used as a pedal point or rhythmic anchor; the
tail provides sequential material. Two-cell construction is intrinsic.

**Answer type:** Depends on where the 1–5 boundary falls relative to
the two halves. If the held note is on 1 and the run reaches 5, tonal
mutation is needed. If the held note is on 5 (dominant answer), it may
be real.

## 6. DANCE — Subject derived from dance rhythm

**Source examples:** BWV 866 (Bb major, WTC I — gigue-like), BWV 858
(F# major, WTC I — dance character), Corelli Op. 5 gigues, Telemann
suite fugal gigues, Handel concerti grossi dance-fugues (minuet-fugues
in Op. 6 Nos. 5, 6, 10).

**Contour:** Shaped by the dance metre. Gigue: wide leaps (6ths, octaves)
in compound time (6/8, 12/8) or notated 4/4 with triplet subdivision.
Minuet-fugue: graceful stepwise with characteristic 3/4 weight on beat
1. Bourrée-fugue: sturdy upbeat figure. The contour follows the dance's
physical gesture.

**Rhythmic profile:** Dictated entirely by the dance idiom. Gigue:
compound quaver groups with characteristic dotted figures. Minuet:
crotchet–crotchet–minim or crotchet–quaver–quaver patterns. The dance
rhythm is non-negotiable — it defines the genre.

**Harmonic implication:** Dance subjects tend to be harmonically simple
and periodic — 2-bar or 4-bar phrase units with clear half-cadence at
midpoint. The harmonic rhythm matches the dance's phrase structure.

**Affect affinity:** Freudigkeit (gigue = playful energy), Zierlich
(minuet = elegant grace), Entschlossenheit (bourrée = vigorous
drive).

**Fragmentation:** Dance subjects fragment into their constituent
rhythmic cells (each bar or half-bar is a unit). Sequential episodes
transpose these cells through the circle of fifths.

**Answer type:** Usually real — dance subjects are melodically simple
and rarely cross the tonic–dominant boundary dramatically.

```

         (Triadic possible but less common in minor fugues)
```

## Mapping: Metre → Archetype constraints

```
4/4:     All archetypes. Subject typically 1–2 bars.
3/4:     Scalar, Dance, Compound. Subject typically 2–4 bars.
6/8:     Dance (gigue), Scalar. Subject typically 2 bars.
2/2:     Scalar, Rhythmic (stile antico). Subject typically 1–2 bars.
```

## Generation Parameters per Archetype

### Scalar
- head_length: 4–8 notes
- max_interval: 2 (stepwise only in head)
- direction: ascending OR descending (chosen by affect)
- range: 5th to octave
- rhythm: even values (density from affect)
- tail: reversal or cadential figure
- tonic_dominant_boundary: explicit (scalar crosses it)

### Triadic
- head_length: 3–5 notes
- intervals: 2, 3, 4 (thirds and their compounds)
- direction: ascending OR descending arpeggio
- range: octave to 10th
- rhythm: long on chord tones, short on fills
- tail: gap-fill (stepwise contrary to arpeggio direction)
- tonic_dominant_boundary: at arpeggio apex/nadir

### Chromatic
- head_length: 4–8 notes
- chromatic_notes: 1–3 (not more, or key is lost)
- chromatic_type: descending lamento, neighbour, approach tone
- range: 4th to 6th (narrow, intense)
- rhythm: long values (gravity)
- tail: resolution to diatonic stability
- tonic_dominant_boundary: often at chromatic inflection point

### Rhythmic
- head_length: 3–6 notes
- pitch_range: 4th to 5th (narrow)
- defining_rhythm: chosen from vocabulary (dotted, repeated, syncopated)
- pitch_content: secondary to rhythm — chord tones + neighbours
- tail: continues rhythmic pattern with wider pitch motion
- tonic_dominant_boundary: ambiguous (advantage for real answer)

### Compound
- head_length: 2–4 notes (static half)
- tail_length: 4–8 notes (kinetic half)
- head_type: held note, repeated note, or slow arpeggio
- tail_type: scale run, sequential figuration
- rhythmic_contrast: long → short (mandatory)
- range: octave+ (the tail covers distance)
- tonic_dominant_boundary: typically between the two halves

### Dance
- head_length: 4–8 notes
- dance_type: gigue, minuet, bourrée (from genre config)
- rhythm: non-negotiable (from dance idiom)
- intervals: wide for gigue, stepwise for minuet
- range: 6th to octave
- phrase_structure: periodic (2 or 4 bar units)
- tonic_dominant_boundary: at phrase midpoint

## What the Generator Does

```
1. Input: affect, genre, mode, metre, seed
2. Select archetype: affect→archetype map, filtered by genre and mode
3. Choose direction: ascending/descending (from affect contour profile)
4. Generate Kopfmotiv:
   - Pitch: within archetype constraints (intervals, range)
   - Rhythm: from archetype rhythmic profile, bound to pitch
   - Start degree: tonic triad member (0, 2, or 4)
5. Generate Fortspinnung:
   - Continues head's energy
   - Archetype-specific rules (gap-fill for triadic, continuation
     for scalar, kinetic half for compound)
6. Generate Kadenz:
   - Resolves to stable degree (1, 3, or 5)
   - May slow rhythmically
7. Validate:
   - Melodic intervals (no 7ths, no tritone outlines)
   - Range within voice limits
   - Duration sums to integer bars
   - At least 2 distinct rhythmic values
   - No unresolved leading tones
8. Analyse:
   - Tonic-dominant boundary position → answer type
   - Fragment points → episode cell catalogue
   - Invertibility check (does inversion preserve contour character?)
9. Output: GeneratedSubject with archetype tag, fragment analysis,
   answer recommendation
```

## Composer-Specific Notes

### Bach
All six archetypes represented across WTC I+II and organ works. Favours
Scalar and Compound for pedagogical fugues (WTC I). Chromatic subjects
concentrated in minor keys. Subject length varies widely (1 bar to 4
bars). Dense motivic content — every note earns its place.

### Handel
Favours Triadic and Rhythmic. Subjects are harmonically direct and
immediately memorable — less contrapuntally intricate than Bach, more
theatrically effective. The Op. 6 concerti grossi subjects often emerge
from simple harmonic gestures (broken chords, repeated notes with
accelerando). Shorter subjects on average.

### Vivaldi
Strongly Rhythmic and Triadic. Concerto fugue subjects are motoric —
driving repeated patterns designed for orchestral texture. Less
melodically complex than Bach or Handel. Strong emphasis on momentum
and physical energy. Subjects tend to be short (1 bar) and
harmonically simple.

### Corelli
Triadic and Scalar, in the sonata da chiesa tradition. Subjects are
vocally conceived even in instrumental works — singable, with moderate
range and stepwise preference. The second movement of a Corelli sonata
is typically a gentle fugue with a lyrical subject. Harmonically
conservative.

### Scarlatti
Few fugues, but the Cat's Fugue (K.30) is pure Chromatic archetype —
bizarre intervals, angular, deliberately strange. An outlier that
influenced Handel (Op. 6 No. 12). Scarlatti's fugal subjects are
idiosyncratic rather than systematic.

### Telemann
Favours "light" fugue (Fugues légères) — Rhythmic and Dance archetypes.
Subjects are galant-influenced, melodically graceful, less
contrapuntally severe than Bach. The solo violin fantasias contain
fugues with strongly-cast subjects designed to imply multiple voices
on a single instrument. French stylistic influence visible in dotted
rhythms and ornamental figures.
