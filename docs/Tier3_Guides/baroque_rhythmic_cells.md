The standard named rhythmic cells, mostly borrowed from poetic metre:

**Iamb** — short-long (semiquaver-quaver). The upbeat gesture. Creates forward motion toward the longer note. Very common as a fugue subject opening — Bach WTC I No.2 in C minor starts with one.

**Trochee** — long-short (quaver-semiquaver). The downbeat gesture. Settling, declarative. The reverse energy of an iamb.

**Lombardic snap** — specifically a short-long where the short is on the beat, against expectation. Semiquaver on the downbeat, longer note following. Vivaldi uses it constantly. Energetic, slightly aggressive.

**Dactyl** — long-short-short (quaver-semiquaver-semiquaver). What you spotted as interesting. Opening, expansive feel — the long note establishes weight, the two shorts tumble forward.

**Anapaest** — short-short-long (semiquaver-semiquaver-quaver). The other one you spotted. Builds momentum into the arrival. Very common in fugue subjects as a propulsive cell.

**Dotted rhythm** — dotted quaver + semiquaver. The French overture style. Majestic, ceremonial. Overdone it becomes pompous.

**Siciliana** — dotted rhythm in compound time (6/8 or 12/8). Lilting, pastoral.

**Tirata** — a run of equal fast notes (four or more semiquavers). Scalar energy, no internal accent. What subject 01 lacks entirely.

**Hemiola** — regrouping beats: three groups of two become two groups of three (or vice versa). Only works in triple/compound time. Common at cadences.

The ones most relevant to your duration generator are the first six — they're the atomic cells from which baroque rhythm is built.
