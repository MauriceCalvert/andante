Below is a **practical, composer-facing procedure** consistent with 18th-century practice, stripped of pedagogical dances and exercises.

---

## 1. Fix the frame (before any notes)

**Decide only this:**

* Key (say C minor or D major).
* Meter (usually 4/4 or 3/4).
* Length: 16–32 bars.
* Number of voices: 2 (keyboard-style).

**Implicit decision:** frequent cadences; no long thematic exposition.

---

## 2. Sketch a cadence plan (silent, but decisive)

Think in **arrival points**, not themes.

Typical 2-voice invention cadence map (example in tonic):

* Bars 4: Half cadence (V)
* Bars 8: Perfect cadence (I)
* Bars 12: Modulating cadence (dominant or relative)
* Bars 16: Strong PAC in tonic

This already determines most bass motion.

---

## 3. Choose a schema chain for the opening

Pick **one** schema that:

* fits imitation,
* spans 2–4 bars,
* ends weakly (so it can repeat).

Canonical choices:

* Prinner (descending bass)
* Fonte (sequential, modulatory)
* Meyer + Cadence
* Romanesca variant (if minor)

This schema *is* your invention's DNA.

---

## 4. Extract the schema skeleton

Write (mentally or on paper):

* bass scale degrees,
* implied upper-voice intervals,
* cadence tones.

Ignore rhythm, figuration, and ornament.

At this point you have:

* no "melody",
* but a correct contrapuntal engine.

---

## 5. Crystallize the subject *from the schema*

Now — and only now — shape a subject:

Constraints:

* Mostly stepwise.
* Clear rhythmic profile (often motoric).
* Begins on a consonance supported by the schema.
* Ends before a strong cadence.

The subject is **one voice's realization** of the schema, not a free tune.

If it cannot invert or answer at the fifth without breaking the harmony, it is wrong.

---

## 6. Immediate imitation

* Answer at the octave or fifth.
* While the second voice states the subject, the first continues the same schema as a countersubject.
* Avoid inventing new material here; the counterpoint is schema-driven filler.

This is why invention textures feel tight.

---

## 7. Develop by schema substitution, not motivic tricks

To continue:

* Keep the *rhythmic/motivic surface*.
* Swap the underlying schema.

Typical moves:

* Prinner → Cadence
* Fonte → Fonte (sequence)
* Meyer → Half cadence → PAC

This gives forward motion without thematic invention.

---

## 8. Modulate briefly, then return

* One short excursion (dominant or relative).
* Use a sequential schema (Fonte/Monte).
* Do not "develop" motivically; let the sequence do the work.

---

## 9. Close with inevitability

Final section:

* Compress the subject rhythmically.
* Tighten cadences.
* End with an explicit schema cadence (no ambiguity).

A good invention sounds *obvious* at the end.

---

## Mental checklist (baroque-correct)

* Bass governs everything.
* Subjects are realizations, not premises.
* Contrast comes from schema choice, not new themes.
* If stuck: cadence sooner.

---

## Appendix A: Counter-Subject Constraints

When composing a counter-subject to accompany the subject, satisfy:

| Constraint | Description |
|------------|-------------|
| Rhythmic complementarity | Fills gaps in subject rhythm |
| Melodic motion | Contrary or oblique motion preferred |
| Invertible counterpoint | Intervals work when voices swap octaves |
| Harmonic consistency | Implies same chord progression as subject |
| Registral bounds | Fits available range without collision |

All these constraints are CSP-expressible.

---

## Appendix B: Bass Line Taxonomy

Three categories, from most to least creative freedom:

**Participative bass** — shares the subject material. The bass is a full participant in the imitative texture. Examples: fugue, invention.

**Characterful support** — has its own identity but doesn't carry the principal strand:
- Motivic bass: characteristic rhythmic/melodic figure
- Ground bass/ostinato: repeating pattern
- Idiomatic figures: walking bass, arpeggios, Alberti, pedal points

**Neutral support** — purely functional. Free counterpoint following voice-leading rules, no memorable identity. Most constrained category.

---

## Appendix C: Constraint Classification

### Hard Constraints (must satisfy)

- Voice-leading rules (no parallel fifths/octaves, resolve leading tones)
- Registral bounds (each voice stays in range)
- Harmonic rhythm (chord changes at specified points)
- Cadence placement (structural points non-negotiable)

### Soft Constraints (prefer to satisfy)

- Contrary motion between outer voices
- Stepwise inner-voice movement
- Even distribution of chord tones
- Rhythmic variety within texture

---

## Appendix D: Period Comparison

For future reference if targeting classical/romantic periods:

| Aspect | Baroque | Classical | Romantic |
|--------|---------|-----------|----------|
| Harmonic vocabulary | Diatonic triads/sevenths | Same + augmented sixths | Extended chords, chromatic |
| Voice-leading | Strict counterpoint | Somewhat relaxed | Flexible, voice-doublings |
| Formal structures | Binary, fugue, ritornello | Sonata, rondo, theme/var | Expanded sonata, free forms |
| Treatment palette | Sequence, inversion, canon | Development, fragmentation | Transformation, leitmotif |
| Texture | Consistent polyphony | Melody + accompaniment | Varied, orchestral |
| Rhythmic flexibility | Steady pulse, hemiola | Phrase regularity | Rubato, irregular phrase |
