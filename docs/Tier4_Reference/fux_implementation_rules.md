# Fux Implementation Rules

Extracted from: Johann Joseph Fux, *Gradus ad Parnassum* (English translation, 18th century edition)

OCR source: gradusadparnassum.txt (64KB, 2k lines)

---

## 1. Foundational Definitions

### Counterpoint Types
- **Simple (Plain) Counterpoint**: Note against note in concords only
- **Figurative Counterpoint**: Notes of different values, concords intermixed with discords
- **Contrapunto sopra il soggetto**: Cantus firmus in lower parts
- **Contrapunto sotto il soggetto**: Cantus firmus in upper parts

### Interval Classifications
| Type | Intervals |
|------|-----------|
| Perfect Concords | Unison, 5th, 8ve |
| Imperfect Concords | 3rd, 6th |
| Discords | 2nd, 4th*, 7th, 9th |

*4th is contextually dissonant against bass

---

## 2. Motion Rules (General)

### Three Types of Motion
1. **Similar (Regular)**: Both voices move same direction
2. **Contrary**: Voices move opposite directions  
3. **Oblique**: One voice stationary, other moves

### Motion Restrictions

| From → To | Similar | Contrary | Oblique |
|-----------|---------|----------|---------|
| Perfect → Perfect | **FORBIDDEN** | Allowed | Allowed |
| Perfect → Imperfect | Allowed | Allowed | Allowed |
| Imperfect → Perfect | **FORBIDDEN** | Allowed | Allowed |
| Imperfect → Imperfect | Allowed | Allowed | Allowed |

### Hidden (Direct) 5ths and 8ves
- Discovered by examining intermediate notes in compound intervals
- Forbidden when similar motion approaches perfect consonance
- **Critical rule**: Avoid approaching unison/8ve by skip when bass ascends by step and upper voice descends by skip (even in contrary motion)

---

## 3. General Composition Rules

1. **Prefer imperfect concords** over perfect (more harmonious)
2. **Notes must conform to key** (modal/tonal coherence)
3. **Avoid cross-relation** (Mi against Fa — tritone)
4. **Maintain regularity, variety, and good melody**
5. **Begin and end with perfect concord**

---

## 4. Five Species of Counterpoint

### Species 1: Note Against Note
- Every note consonant
- CF in lower part: penultimate note takes major 6th
- CF in upper part: penultimate note takes minor 3rd

### Species 2: Two Notes Against One (Binary)
- First note = Concord (accented)
- Second note = Concord OR Discord (unaccented)
- **Discords only in stepwise motion** (passing)
- **Skips require concords**

Penultimate bar rules:
- CF lower: 5th then major 6th
- CF upper: 5th then minor 3rd

**Avoid**: Consecutive 3rd-skips in similar motion → hidden 5ths/8ves

### Species 3: Four Notes Against One
- Notes 1, 3, 5 = Concords (in stepwise passage)
- Notes 2, 4 = Discords (passing)
- **Alternative**: Notes 2, 4 consonant, note 3 dissonant (fills 3rd)

**Nota Cambiata**: Skip from discord to concord (3rd skip from note 2 to 3)
- Licensed exception to stepwise dissonance resolution

### Species 4: Syncopation (Ligatures)
- **Syncopation of Concords**: Both parts consonant
- **Syncopation of Discords**: Unaccented = concord, accented = discord

Dissonance resolution (CF in lower part):
| Dissonance | Resolution |
|------------|------------|
| 2nd | Unison |
| 4th | 3rd |
| 7th | 6th |
| 9th | 8ve |

Dissonance resolution (CF in upper part):
| Dissonance | Resolution |
|------------|------------|
| 2nd | 3rd |
| 4th | 5th |
| 7th | 8ve |
| 9th | 10th |

**Key principle**: Discords are retardations of concords — "resolve by falling one degree"

**Forbidden**: 
- Unison → 2nd (syncopation) — creates parallel unisons
- 8ve → 9th (syncopation) — creates parallel 8ves

### Species 5: Florid Counterpoint
- Mixture of all species
- Variety of rhythms, passing tones, suspensions
- **Avoid**: Two crotchets at bar beginning without syncopation (sounds like ending)
- Maintain continual motion

## 5. Three-Part Composition

### Harmonic Triad
- Introduce in every bar when possible
- May substitute 6th or 8ve for melody/voice-leading reasons
- Fifth below, Third above (natural division of 8ve)

### Voice-Leading Extensions
- Same rules apply to middle parts as extreme parts
- In 3+ parts: rules cannot always be strictly observed to avoid greater irregularities
- Ascending 6ths on accented beats more displeasing than on unaccented

### Practical Allowances
- 5th to 8ve in similar motion: allowed at conclusions when unavoidable
- Skip of 3rd allowed for completing triad or avoiding parallel 5ths

---

## 6. Four-Part Composition

### Doubling Principles
- Fourth part doubles a note of the triad
- **Natural order**: 5th below, 3rd above (harmonic series)
- Maintain proper reference between all parts, not just extremes

### Syncopation Accompaniment
- Third part takes same concord as if no syncopation existed
- Parallel 5ths covered by syncopation: allowed in composition

---

## 7. Imitation and Fugue

### Imitation
- Following part repeats preceding part's intervals
- Not confined to whole/half tone species or key
- May begin on any interval
- Better suited to middle of composition (less key restriction)

### Fugue
- Strict repetition: same intervals, same whole/half tone species
- **Confined to key rules**
- Key determined by 4th and 5th within the 8ve

**Answer rules**:
- If subject spans 5th → answer confined to 4th
- If subject spans 4th → answer confined to 5th
- Begin only on key-defining intervals: Unison, 5th, 8ve

**Fugue structure (2 parts)**:
1. First part states subject
2. Second part answers at 4th or 5th
3. First cadence in 5th of key
4. Resume subject in different interval
5. Second cadence in 3rd of key
6. Stretto (closer imitation)
7. Final cadence

### Three-Part Fugue
- No formal cadence ending with sharp 3rd (cannot bring in subject)
- Both formal and fictitious cadences allowed on 5th, 3rd, and other degrees

## 8. Double Counterpoint

### Types (by inversion interval)
- **At the 8ve**: Most practical, commonly used
- **At the 10th**: Useful
- **At the 12th**: Useful
- (Others: 3rd, 4th, 5th, 6th — confined, less practical)

### General Rules
1. Both original and inverted version must be correct
2. Do not exceed limits prescribed for each type
3. Inversion transforms intervals:
   - At 8ve: 1↔8, 2↔7, 3↔6, 4↔5
   - 5ths become 4ths (dissonant) — avoid

## 9. Fugue Answer Rules (Ch. 45)

### General Principles
1. Answer melody must exactly correspond with guide
2. True modulation/key conformity required
3. Key note (1) and 5th must answer each other on first/last notes
4. In skips (beginning or middle): 1 and 5 answer each other

### Melody vs. Key
- When conflict exists, melody is preferred
- Chromatic subjects: reduce to diatonic, form answer, then re-chromaticize

### Syncopation Placement by Meter
| Meter | Dissonance positions |
|-------|---------------------|
| Common time (Binary) | 1st (accented) only |
| Quick (Presto, 4 crotchets) | 1st and 3rd |
| Slow (Adagio) | Every crotchet |

## 10. Implementation Priority for Andante

### Phase 1: Core Voice-Leading
1. **Motion detection**: Similar/contrary/oblique classification
2. **Parallel check**: 5ths, 8ves, unisons (open and hidden)
3. **Interval classification**: Perfect vs imperfect concords

### Phase 2: Dissonance Handling
1. **Passing tone validation**: Stepwise approach and departure
2. **Suspension patterns**: Preparation → suspension → resolution
3. **Resolution direction**: Always descending by step

### Phase 3: Species Integration
1. **First species**: Note-against-note voice leading
2. **Fourth species**: Syncopation/suspension chains
3. **Fifth species**: Mixed rhythmic values

### Phase 4: Fugue/Imitation
1. **Subject/answer relationship**: 5th↔4th constraint
2. **Entry intervals**: Unison, 5th, 8ve
3. **Cadence placement**: 5th, 3rd of key

## 12. Key Differences from Later Practice

1. **Modal foundation**: Fux's examples in church modes, not major/minor
2. **Stricter dissonance**: No accented passing tones (except nota cambiata)
3. **No chord progressions**: Voice-leading primary, harmony emergent
4. **Contrapuntal texture**: All voices equally important
