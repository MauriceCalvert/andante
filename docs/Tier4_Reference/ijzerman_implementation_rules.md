## 1. Voice-Leading Fundamentals

### Parallel Motion Rules
- **Parallel 3rds and 6ths**: Allowed and common; form basis of scale realization
- **Parallel 5ths, 8ves, unisons**: Forbidden — destroy voice independence
- **Parallel 5/3 chords**: Forbidden due to inherent parallel 5ths between voices
- **Parallel 6/3 chords**: Allowed throughout — the "fauxbourdon"
- **Exception**: Orchestral doubling (e.g., flute doubling violin) not considered parallel motion

### Invertible Counterpoint at the Octave
- 3rd ↔ 6th (both consonant)
- 2nd ↔ 7th (both dissonant)
- Avoid 5ths (become 4ths when inverted, treated as dissonance)
- 3-2-3 suspension relates to 6-7-6 by invertible counterpoint

### Melodic Minor Rule
- Raised 6th and 7th (melodic minor) occur in ascending motion
- Natural 6th and 7th occur in descending motion

## 2. Suspension Patterns

### Core Principle
- Dissonance occurs on strong beat
- Preparation on preceding weak beat (consonance)
- Resolution on following weak beat (stepwise down)

### Two-Part Suspensions
| Pattern | Voice | Context |
|---------|-------|---------|
| 7-6 | Upper voice tied | Descending bass |
| 2-3 | Lower voice (bass) tied | Descending upper voice |
| 7-6-8 | Upper | Compound cadence |
| 2-3-1 | Lower | Compound cadence |

### Three-Part Suspensions
| Pattern | Chord progression | Notes |
|---------|-------------------|-------|
| 5/3 → 7/5/3 → 6/3 | Monte ascending | 5-6 alternation |
| 5/3 → 7/3 → 6/3 | 7-6 Fauxbourdon descending | |
| 5/3 → 4/2 → 6/3 | Tied Bass | 2-6 resolution |

### Key Rule
- 7-6 and 2-3 progressions occur in **descending motion exclusively**
- Dissonance always resolves stepwise down

## 3. Cadence Taxonomy (Appendix II)

### Two-Part Cadences

| Type | Interval progression | Bass motion | Notes |
|------|---------------------|-------------|-------|
| Simple tenor | 6-8 (6#-8 minor) | 2-1 step | Soprano 2-1 |
| Simple discant | 3-1 | 2-1 step | Leading tone 7-1 |
| Compound tenor | 7-6-8 | 2-1 step | Suspension in upper voice |
| Compound discant | 2-3-1 | 2-1 step | Suspension in bass |
| Double | Simple + compound | 2-1 step | Twofold leading-tone resolution |

### Three-Part Cadences

**Tenor cadence** (bass 2-1 step):
- Simple: 6/3 → 6/3 on 2-1
- Compound: 5/3 → 7/5/3 → 6/3
- Final 6/3 = incomplete 5/3 chord

**Bass-leap cadence** (bass 5-1 leap):
- Simple: 5/3 → 5/3
- Compound: 5/3 → 6/4 → 5/3
- Galant: 4-2-5-1 bass with 6/4/3 on 2

### Cadence Qualities
- **Perfect**: Both bass and soprano end on tonic (1)
- **Imperfect**: Soprano ends on 3 OR bass ends on 5
- **Deceptive**: Bass moves to 6 instead of 1
- **Half**: Ends on 5 (dominant)

### Phrygian Cadence
- Based on half-tone bass step (b2-1 in minor)
- Special case for Baroque slow movements

## 4. Rule of the Octave

### Structure
- **Pentachord**: 1-2-3-4-5 (lower half)
- **Tetrachord**: 5-6-7-8 (upper half)
- Stable anchors: 1 and 5
- Mobile degrees: 2, 3, 4, 6, 7

### Three-Part Rule of the Octave

| Degree | Ascending | Descending | Notes |
|--------|-----------|------------|-------|
| 1 | 5/3 | 5/3 | Tonic, stable |
| 2 | 6/3 | 6/3 | First inversion |
| 3 | 6/3 | 6/3 or 5/3 | |
| 4 | 6/3 | 6/3 | |
| 5 | 5/3 | 5/3 | Dominant, stable |
| 6 | 6/3 | 6/3 | |
| 7 | 6/3 | 6/5/3 or 6/3 | Leading tone |

### Key Principles
- Scale degree 4 ascending takes 6/3 (not 5/3 — would create parallel 5ths)
- Scale degree 7 takes 6/3 (diminished triad in first inversion)
- Direction matters: ascending vs descending may differ on same degree

## 5. Galant Schemata

### 5.1 Romanesca

**Leaping Romanesca** (Pachelbel Canon):
- Bass: 1-5-6-3-4-1-4-5 (4th down, 2nd up pattern)
- Chords: 5/3 alternating complete/incomplete
- Four segments, each a third lower

**Stepwise Romanesca**:
- Bass: descending scale 8-7-6-5-4-3-2-1
- Chords: alternating 5/3 and 6/3
- Related to leaping by invertible counterpoint

### 5.2 Quiescenza
- Pedal point (usually on 1 or 5)
- Chord progression over static bass
- Marks beginning or arrival point

### 5.3 Prinner
- Bass: 6-5-4-3 (stepwise descent to tonic)
- Upper voice: 4-3-2-1 in parallel 10ths with bass
- Or: 6-5-4-3 divided between upper voices
- Function: relaxing response to energetic opening
- Ends with weak cadence
- 5/3 chord on 4 replaces RO's 6/3 — distinctive Prinner feature

### 5.4 Monte Romanesca
- Combines ascending Monte with descending Romanesca elements
- Bass: 4th up, 2nd down pattern (ascending overall)
- Can include suspensions creating canon between upper voices

### 5.5 Fenaroli
- Bass: circles around tonic (1-7-1-2-1 or similar)
- Upper voice: pedal on 5
- When repeated: creates simple canon between bass and upper voice
- Named after Neapolitan master Fedele Fenaroli

### 5.6 Folia
- Harmonic/melodic framework for variations
- Minor mode
- Bass: 1-5-1-7-3-7-1-5 (typical pattern)
- One of oldest schemata (16th century origin)

## 6. Sequential Patterns

### Fifth Down Fourth Up
- Bass: alternating 5th down, 4th up (net descent by step)
- Chords: succession of 5/3 chords
- Three-part version closely related to:
  - Two-part 7-3 progression
  - 7-6 Fauxbourdon
- Often embellished with diminutions and imitations

### Third Down Second Up
- Similar principle, different intervals
- Creates different harmonic rhythm

### Monte (Ascending)
- Bass: stepwise ascending
- Chords: alternating 5/3 and 6/3
- 5-6 pattern avoids parallel 5ths

### 7-6 Fauxbourdon (Descending)
- Mirrors Monte in descent
- Alternating 5/3 and 7/5/3 → 6/3

## 7. Specific Voice-Leading Solutions

### Avoiding Parallel 5ths in Cadences
- Deceptive cadence (5-6 bass): middle voice must descend stepwise
- Insert passing tone in bass to break direct 5th-to-5th motion
- Use 6/3 chord to interpolate between 5/3 chords

### Tied Bass Treatment
- Alternation of 4/2 and 6/3 chords
- 4/2 chord on tied bass tone (strong beat)
- 6/3 chord on weak beat
- Three-part variant of two-part 2-3 schema

### Double Cadence Structure
- Four chords on sustained 5 (dominant pedal)
- Can start with 5/3 or 6/3
- Sequence: initial → 6/3 → 5/3 → 5/3
- Features twofold leading-tone resolution

### Galant Cadence
- Bass: 4-2-5-1
- Scale degree 2 supports 6/4/3 (not RO's 6/3)
- Parallel 6ths or 3rds in upper voices stereotypical
- Can be simple (5/3 on 5) or compound (6/4 on 5)

## 8. Chromaticism

### Fonte
- Chromatic schema for modulation
- Typically moves from minor to relative major

### Chromatic Monte
- Ascending sequence with chromatic alterations
- Each segment tonicizes next step

### Lamento (Chromatic Tetrachord)
- Descending chromatic bass: 8-#7-7-#6-6-5
- Traditional grief/lament affect
- Multiple realization options

### Chain of Dominants
- Each chord is dominant of the next
- Creates extended tonicization sequence
- 7th chords throughout

## 9. Implementation Priority for Andante

### Phase 1: Core Patterns
1. **Cadences**: Simple, compound, double (all three types)
2. **Rule of the Octave**: Three-part ascending/descending
3. **Suspension patterns**: 7-6, 2-3, tied bass

### Phase 2: Schemata
1. **Prinner**: Most common galant response
2. **Romanesca**: Both leaping and stepwise
3. **Monte/Fauxbourdon**: Ascending/descending sequences
4. **Fenaroli**: Tonic prolongation

### Phase 3: Advanced
1. **Galant cadence**: 4-2-5-1 with 6/4/3
2. **Folia**: Variation framework
3. **Chromatic patterns**: Fonte, Lamento
