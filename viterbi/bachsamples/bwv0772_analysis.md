# BWV 772 Analysis — C Major Invention (Bach)

22 bars, 2 voices (tracks 4 and 5 in .note file). The prototypical
two-part invention.

## Texture Rotation

The single most distinctive structural feature. Bach changes texture
every 2–4 bars across at least five distinct modes:

| Bars  | Texture         | Description                                      |
|-------|-----------------|--------------------------------------------------|
| 2–3   | Subject + free  | Exposition: one voice leads, other fills          |
| 4–7   | RUN-U / SLOW-L  | Episode: soprano sixteenths, bass in eighths      |
| 8–9   | Subject in bass | Voice swap: bass leads, soprano accompanies       |
| 10–13 | SLOW-U / RUN-L  | Inverted episode: bass runs, soprano in eighths   |
| 14–15 | DENSE-BOTH      | Climactic convergence, both voices busy           |
| 16–19 | HOLD + RUN      | Half-note exchange: one voice sustains, other runs|
| 20–22 | Return to dense | Final buildup                                     |
| 23    | Whole notes     | Resolution                                        |

The half-note exchange (bars 16–19) is the most distinctive sound.
One voice holds a half note while the other plays the subject head in
sixteenths. Then they swap every half bar. Four bars of this creates
a rocking, breathing pattern unlike anything else in the piece.

## Contrary-Motion Episodes

Bach's episodes (bars 4–5, 10–11) aren't just subject fragments
transposed downward. Both voices have subject-derived material moving
in opposite directions simultaneously — one ascending, one descending
by step. This creates real development, not mechanical transposition.

## Rhythmic Contrast Between Voices

When one voice has the subject (sixteenths), the other plays in
eighths — half the rhythmic density. This 2:1 ratio creates textural
relief. The voices never run at the same density for more than a bar
or two.

## Free Counterpoint is Thematically Derived

When Bach's voices aren't playing subject/answer/CS, they play
recognisable fragments — the opening scale-run inverted, the mordent
figure sequenced, the tail truncated. "Free" counterpoint is free of
the subject's complete statement but not free of its motivic DNA.

## Chromatic Approach Tones

F#, C#, G#, Bb appear as secondary leading tones as Bach modulates
through G major, A minor, D minor, F major, C major. These aren't
just key changes; they're chromatic inflections within melodic lines.
The raised 7th of each new key area appears before the cadential
arrival, signalling the modulation to the ear.

## Variable Note Density

Bach's bars range from 7 to 19 notes in the upper voice. When one
voice holds, note count drops; when both run, it rises. This is a
consequence of texture rotation — density varies because texture
varies.

## Cadence Grows From Material

Bars 21–22 weave subject fragments into the cadential descent. The
soprano plays the subject head ascending to C6, then the mordent
figure, then resolves through the same intervals as the subject.
The cadence sounds like it belongs to this piece, not like a
formulaic ending pasted on.

## Mid-Bar Answer Entry

The answer enters at beat 3 of bar 2, during the subject's statement.
The exposition isn't subject-block-then-answer-block; it's overlapping
from the start. The subject's trill on C5 is still sounding when the
bass begins the answer on C3.
