# Stretto in Bach's fugues: a comprehensive analytical survey

**Bach's use of stretto — the overlapping of fugue subject entries before the previous statement finishes — represents one of the most sophisticated achievements in Western contrapuntal art.** Across the Well-Tempered Clavier, The Art of Fugue, and dozens of other works, Bach treats stretto not merely as an ornamental device but as a structural principle that drives entire compositions. Scholars from Ebenezer Prout in 1891 to computational musicologists in the 2010s have attempted to systematize this technique, revealing that Bach's stretto writing exhausts combinatorial possibilities with a rigor approaching mathematical completeness. The emerging picture is of a composer who designed subjects *from the outset* to support canonic self-overlap, then deployed stretto with extraordinary structural intelligence — typically building from loose to tight imitation to create trajectories of intensification.

## How stretto works: mechanics of canonic self-overlap

Stretto (Italian, from *stringere*, "to tighten"; German *Engführung*, "close leading") occurs when a second or subsequent entry of the fugue subject begins before the previous entry has completed, creating contrapuntal overlap. The technique is essentially a **temporary canon on the fugue subject** — since every entry imitates the same melody, their temporal overlap produces canonic texture embedded within the fugue's fabric. The two chief effects, as analyst Siglind Bruhn observes, are *acceleration* (the time to complete a full cycle of entries compresses) and *increased density* (multiple voices simultaneously carry primary thematic material).

The taxonomy of stretto types reflects several independent variables. **Regular stretto** preserves the conventional tonic-dominant pitch relationship of subject and answer; **partial stretto** involves entries that break off into free counterpoint after the next voice enters (Prout's rule: the *last* entering voice must always complete the subject). **Stretto maestrale** — the "masterly stretto" — requires all voices to carry the subject to completion, forming a strict canon. **Stretto by inversion** pairs the original subject with its melodic mirror; **stretto by augmentation** doubles note values so one voice moves at half speed; **stretto by diminution** halves them for double speed. These transformations can combine: Contrapunctus VII of The Art of Fugue simultaneously presents the subject in original form, inverted, augmented, and diminished.

Two further variables matter enormously. The **time interval of entry** (how many beats before the next voice enters) determines stretto tightness — BWV 846 progressively compresses from six beats to one. The **pitch interval of entry** (fifth, octave, fourth, third, etc.) determines harmonic character. As Prout notes in §248 of his *Fugue*, interest typically increases when later stretti are either in more parts or at shorter distances than earlier ones — a principle of *progressive tightening* that Bach follows with remarkable consistency.

## A catalog of stretto across the Well-Tempered Clavier

Bach's 48 fugues of the WTC span the full spectrum from stretto-saturated to completely stretto-free, providing a natural laboratory for understanding the technique.

**Book I's paradigmatic stretto fugue is No. 1 in C major (BWV 846).** Of approximately 22 complete subject entries in just 27 measures, **17 are involved in stretto**. The fugue has no countersubject — stretto is its *sole* developmental device. Beginning at measure 7, immediately after the four-voice exposition, stretto entries progressively tighten from two-beat intervals to the climactic one-beat entries of the closing stretto maestrale. The subject's design enables this: it opens with a sustained half note (providing harmonic stability for the incoming entry), proceeds in stepwise eighth-note motion (minimizing dissonant collisions), and stays within a narrow range. As Gilead Bar-Elli of Hebrew University observes, "this barrage of strettos in such a short fugue is outstanding."

**No. 22 in B♭ minor (BWV 867)** pushes stretto even further in a five-voice texture. Stretto begins *within the exposition itself* — the first two entries already overlap. Nearly every subsequent entry but one involves stretto, culminating in a "hyperstretto" where all five voices overlap offset by only a single note. The subject is deliberately short (a necessity for five-voice stretto to remain manageable), built around a distinctive pair of descending half notes from tonic to dominant that serves as an aural anchor amid extreme contrapuntal density.

**No. 4 in C♯ minor (BWV 849)**, the monumental five-voice triple fugue, deploys stretto as a climactic structural device. After extended development of three separate subjects, **10 successively chained entries** of the tolling subject cascade across all five voices near the conclusion (approximately measures 92–101), with a simultaneous double stretto on the cross motif — one of the most dramatic passages in all of Bach.

**No. 6 in D minor (BWV 851)** demonstrates stretto by inversion: five distinct stretto passages appear *exclusively in the second half*, combining the original subject with its inversion at intervals of three quarter notes, and building from two-voice to three-voice stretto. **No. 8 in E♭ minor (BWV 853)** introduces progressive stretto with augmentation, combining original, inverted, and augmented entries across its three voices.

Book II offers equally remarkable examples. **No. 5 in D major (BWV 874)** parallels BWV 846 as a dedicated stretto fugue — a short two-bar subject with no countersubject generates approximately 100 motivic appearances in 50 bars through "condensed strettos" of increasing density. **No. 3 in C♯ major (BWV 872)** places stretto *within a three-bar exposition*, immediately introducing inversion and diminution by measure 4. **No. 2 in C minor (BWV 871)** features quadruple stretto — the subject in all four voices simultaneously — and stretto combining the subject with its own augmentation.

Critically, several WTC fugues use **no stretto whatsoever**: No. 2 in C minor (BWV 847), No. 3 in C♯ major (BWV 848), and No. 24 in B minor (BWV 869, with its all-twelve-tone chromatic subject). These absences are as instructive as the presences — they correlate directly with subject properties that resist canonic self-overlap.

## What makes a subject amenable to stretto

The scholarly literature converges on several properties that predict a subject's "stretto-ability." Prout states the principle most directly in §51 of *Fugue*: **"Subjects should be designed for stretto"** — meaning the best fugue composers plan stretto possibilities from the moment of subject invention.

The most important enabling feature is **an opening on longer note values**. A sustained note at the subject's head provides harmonic stability over which the incoming entry can begin without creating immediate dissonance. BWV 846's half-note C, BWV 867's half-note pair, and the Art of Fugue's whole-note D all exemplify this principle. A rhythmic profile that *accelerates* from head to tail (long → short) creates natural space for overlap, as the active continuation of one voice coincides with the stable opening of the next.

**Predominantly stepwise motion** (or small intervals) facilitates consonant overlap — stepwise melodies are statistically more likely to form acceptable intervals when superimposed on a delayed copy of themselves. **Strong tonic-dominant outlining** keeps overlapping entries harmonically compatible within the key. Peter Schubert and Christoph Neidhofer's *Baroque Counterpoint* offers a systematic method: "Using Reduction to Examine a Subject for Stretto Possibilities" — reducing the subject to its harmonic skeleton and testing whether overlapping copies produce acceptable progressions.

Subjects that *resist* stretto share opposite traits: **chromaticism** (BWV 869's twelve-tone subject creates unavoidable dissonances when overlapped), **length** (the Royal Theme of the Musical Offering is too long and chromatic for tight stretto even in six voices), and **strong modulatory tendency** (subjects that move quickly to the dominant create tonal confusion when stacked). Notably, **monothematic fugues** (those lacking a countersubject) are strongly correlated with heavy stretto usage — BWV 846, 867, 871, and 874 all lack countersubjects, because stretto *is* their developmental engine.

## Scholarly frameworks from Prout to Lewin

The analytical literature on stretto spans more than a century and ranges from descriptive cataloging to mathematical formalization.

**Ebenezer Prout's** *Fugue* (1891) remains the most comprehensive traditional taxonomy. His Chapter 15 (§§244–280) classifies stretto by number of voices, completeness of entries, temporal interval, pitch interval, transformational type (inversion, augmentation, diminution), and degree of canonic strictness. His companion volume *Fugal Analysis* applies these categories fugue-by-fugue across the WTC and remains a ground-truth reference used even in 21st-century computational studies. **Donald Tovey's** analyses — his *Forty-Eight Preludes and Fugues* (1924) and *A Companion to 'The Art of Fugue'* (1931) — are more essayistic and performance-oriented, noting stretto's contribution to dramatic pacing and formal structure. **Alfred Mann's** *The Study of Fugue* (1958) provides historical context by tracing stretto pedagogy through the treatises of Fux, Marpurg, Martini, and Albrechtsberger.

More recent scholarship has introduced formal analytical frameworks. **David A. Sheldon's** "The Stretto Principle: Some Thoughts on Fugue as Form" (*Journal of Musicology*, 1990) argues that stretto should be understood as a **form-defining principle** — the progressive tightening of imitative entries constitutes the fugue's fundamental formal dynamic, not merely a local ornament. **William Renwick's** *Analyzing Fugue: A Schenkerian Approach* (1995) demonstrates how stretto passages create tonal meaning through voice-leading structure, noting that "the underlying tonal meaning of such passages cannot be determined completely on a theoretical basis."

The most mathematically rigorous approach applies **David Lewin's** Generalized Interval System (GIS) to stretto. Scott Murphy's landmark article "A Remarkable Non-Duplication of Stretto in J.S. Bach's 'The Art of Fugue'" (*Music Theory and Analysis*, 2018) identifies five types of transformations that preserve a stretto's contrapuntal propriety: octave transposition of a single line, transposition into another key, invertible counterpoint at the octave, mirror inversion, and augmentation/diminution. Murphy demonstrates that among the **22 stretti formed by 26 entries in Contrapunctus VII, not one is related to any other by any combination of these five transformations** — proving mathematically that Bach achieved complete non-duplication, an extraordinary compositional feat.

**Konstantinos Alevizos** developed a four-parameter system analyzing each stretto by (1) distance of entry, (2) raw material used (rectus/inversus), (3) inversion technique, and (4) dux/comes position, published in detailed studies of Contrapuncti V and VI (*Revue musicale OICRM*, 2019–2020).

## Computational musicology and the stretto detection gap

The most significant computational fugue analysis project is by the **Algomus group** (Giraud, Groult, Levé, and Leguy), whose "Computational Fugue Analysis" (*Computer Music Journal*, 2015) applies algorithmic subject and countersubject detection across 36 fugues from Bach's WTC I and Shostakovich's Op. 87. Using diatonic interval similarity with strict length matching on symbolic scores with separated voices, they detect subject occurrences, countersubjects, harmonic sequences, cadences, and pedals. Their interactive visualizations at algomus.fr compare computed analyses against reference annotations from Bruhn, Prout, and Keller.

However, **explicit stretto detection as a named algorithmic module has not yet been published as a standalone tool**. The Algomus system detects overlapping subject entries as a byproduct of subject detection — when two detected subject instances overlap temporally, stretto can be inferred — but no dedicated stretto classifier has been formalized. This represents a clear gap in the literature. Other computational tools applied to Bach include **Humdrum Toolkit** analyses of WTC fugues (University of Alicante project with Tableau visualization dashboards) and various pattern-matching algorithms (Smith & Medina 2001; Lartillot's "motivic pattern mining" 2007).

Machine learning approaches to Bach have focused on adjacent problems rather than stretto directly: Kranenburg (2007) used nearest-neighbor classifiers for authorship attribution of disputed organ fugues; Dirst and Weigend (1993) applied statistical methods to Contrapunctus XIV's completion; and Paz et al. used Shannon entropy measures to compare completions of The Art of Fugue's unfinished final fugue. **No large-scale quantitative corpus study has yet systematically measured stretto parameters — frequency, entry interval, voice count, formal position — across Bach's entire fugal output**, though the Algomus ground-truth dataset (~1,000 annotated segments) provides infrastructure for such research. Mark Richard Ellis's 1980 Nottingham dissertation, "Linear Aspects of the Fugues of J.S. Bach's 'The Well-Tempered Clavier': A Quantitative Survey," represents an early quantitative effort but predates modern computational methods.

## The Art of Fugue as systematic stretto laboratory

The Art of Fugue (BWV 1080) contains the most systematic exploration of stretto in the entire Western musical canon. The work's main subject — D–A–F–D–C♯–D, **contained within the span of a perfect fifth** and deliberately designed for maximal combinatorial potential — generates over 57 stretti across the 14 contrapuncti alone. Christoph Wolff characterized the governing idea as "an exploration in depth of the contrapuntal possibilities inherent in a single musical subject."

The three designated stretto fugues (Contrapuncti V–VII) form a progression of increasing complexity. **Contrapunctus V** introduces bidirectional stretto, systematically exploring every possible time distance: entries at 12 beats, then 6, 4, 2, and finally 0 beats (simultaneous "hyperstretto"). Bach achieves **11 different stretto configurations**, varying entry distance and rectus/inversus assignment. **Contrapunctus VI** ("in Stylo Francese") adds diminution — halving note values so the subject sounds at double speed — creating four forms (rectus, inversus, rectus diminished, inversus diminished) combined in French overture style. As teoria.com's analysis observes, this fugue "does not seem to have an exposition — it is a collection of stretti."

**Contrapunctus VII** ("per Augmentationem et Diminutionem") is the crowning achievement, deploying **six simultaneous versions** of the theme: rectus and inversus at original speed, both in diminution (double speed), and both in augmentation (half speed). Davitt Moroney's vivid description captures the effect: three simultaneous time-levels — "twice as slow, original speed, and twice as fast — all at once, often with one or more strands heard upside down." The augmented version rises steadily through the four voices like a grandfather figure, while normal-speed versions "scurry about in every direction" and diminished versions "rush all over the place." Of its 26 complete entries forming 22 stretti, Murphy's GIS analysis proves that **every single stretto is unique** under all standard contrapuntal transformations — a feat of exhaustive non-duplication that suggests Bach was thinking in combinatorial terms centuries before formal combinatorics.

The macro-structure mirrors a single fugue's form: Contrapuncti I–IV (simple fugues) serve as "exposition"; V–VII (stretto fugues) as "exaltation of the subject"; VIII–XI (double and triple fugues) as developmental combination; XII–XIII (mirror fugues) as vertical inversion; and the unfinished XIV as intended culmination.

## Conclusion

Three insights emerge from this survey that advance beyond standard textbook accounts. First, stretto is not merely a device but a **form-generating principle** — in dedicated stretto fugues like BWV 846, 867, and the Art of Fugue's central triptych, it replaces countersubject development entirely and becomes the fugue's structural logic. Second, Bach's subject design was **prospectively stretto-oriented**: the correlation between specific subject properties (sustained opening, stepwise motion, diatonic simplicity, brevity) and stretto density is strong enough that scholars from Prout onward have argued subjects were reverse-engineered from their canonic possibilities. Third, a significant **computational gap** persists: despite the Algomus group's foundational work on algorithmic fugue analysis and Murphy's rigorous GIS-based stretto formalization, no one has yet combined these approaches into a systematic, corpus-wide quantitative study of stretto across Bach's complete fugal output — measuring frequency, entry intervals, voice count, transformational type, and formal position. Such a study, now feasible with existing tools and annotated datasets, would represent a major contribution to computational musicology.
